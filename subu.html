01 — Introduction

What is Biotechnology & Why Does Patent Law Matter?

Defining Biotechnology

Biotechnology is the application of biological systems, living organisms, or their derivatives to develop products, processes, and technologies that benefit human beings. In its broadest sense, it spans from ancient fermentation techniques (Egyptians used yeasts in bread and wine-making around 4000 B.C.) to modern CRISPR gene editing.

The Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) defines it as: "any technological application that uses biological systems, living organisms, or derivatives thereof, to make or modify products or processes for specific use."

Traditional Biotechnology

Fermentation, selective breeding, cheese production, yeast-based processes — applications of biology without genetic engineering. Practised for millennia before formal IP systems existed.

Modern Biotechnology

Recombinant DNA technology, gene editing (CRISPR), monoclonal antibodies, stem cell therapy, bioinformatics, synthetic biology — the primary domain of biotech patents today.

Defining a Patent

A patent is an exclusive statutory right granted by the government to an inventor for a limited period (generally 20 years from the filing date), in exchange for full public disclosure of the invention. It gives the patent holder the right to exclude others from making, using, selling, or importing the patented invention without permission.

Core Bargain of Patent Law: The inventor discloses the invention to the public; the state grants a temporary monopoly. After the patent expires, the knowledge enters the public domain freely.

02 — Need & Rationale

Why Do We Need Patents in Biotechnology?

Economic Justification

Cost Recovery

Developing a single biologic drug can cost upwards of $2 billion with a 10–15 year development window. Patents allow companies to recoup enormous upfront costs by charging premium prices during the exclusivity period — without which the industry could not sustain itself.

Investment Protection & Venture Capital

Without patent protection, investors would be unwilling to fund early-stage biotech research. Patents serve as collateral assets — making startups investable by demonstrating protected competitive advantage that justifies the risk.

Technology Transfer — The Bayh-Dole Model

Patents facilitate licensing arrangements enabling technology to flow from universities and research institutions to commercial entities. The US Bayh-Dole Act (1980) spurred unprecedented university-industry collaboration and is widely emulated globally.

Role in Promoting R&D

"The patent system added the fuel of interest to the fire of genius." — Abraham Lincoln

Patents incentivize innovation through: (1) Disclosure requirement — expanding the collective pool of scientific knowledge so competitors can design around patents; (2) First-mover advantage — rewarding risk-takers who invest in novel therapies; (3) Sequential innovation — published patent literature becomes a roadmap for follow-on research by others.

Example: Genentech's 1982 patent on recombinant human insulin (produced in bacteria) transformed diabetes treatment globally and catalyzed the entire biopharmaceutical industry — demonstrating the power of biotech patents when functioning as intended.
03 — Patentability Criteria

Criteria for Patentability in Biotechnology

For any biotech invention to be patentable, it must satisfy three universal criteria recognised under TRIPS (Art. 27) and most national patent laws.

① Novelty

The invention must not have been disclosed to the public anywhere in the world before the filing date — assessed by prior art (any earlier publication, patent, or public use).

Biotech Example: Isolating and characterising a protein from a plant never described in prior publications can satisfy novelty. Merely discovering that a naturally occurring substance exists is not novel if its properties were already known.

India (Section 2(1)(l), Patents Act 1970): An invention is not novel if anticipated by prior publication or prior claiming.

② Inventive Step (Non-Obviousness)

The invention must not be obvious to a person skilled in the art (PSITA) in view of the prior art. In India this is "inventive step" (Sec. 2(1)(ja)); in the US it is "non-obviousness" (35 USC §103).

Biotech Example: Creating a novel CRISPR delivery mechanism solving off-target editing problems — a solution a skilled molecular biologist would not have arrived at from existing literature alone — satisfies inventive step.

Key Test (US): Graham v. John Deere Co. (1966) — scope and content of prior art, differences therefrom, level of ordinary skill in the pertinent art.

③ Industrial Applicability (Utility)

The invention must be capable of being made or used in some kind of industry. The invention must have a specific, substantial, and credible utility.

Biotech Example: A gene sequence coding for a therapeutic protein useful in treating cancer satisfies industrial applicability. A randomly sequenced DNA fragment with no known function does not.

Historical Problem: During the Human Genome Project era, many EST (Expressed Sequence Tag) patent applications were filed without demonstrated utility — correctly rejected.

Additional Requirement — India Section 3(d): For known substances, a mere new form that does not result in enhanced efficacy is not patentable. This provision — made famous by Novartis v. Union of India (2013) regarding imatinib (Gleevec) — is India's powerful anti-evergreening shield.
04 — Subject Matter

Patentable vs. Non-Patentable Subject Matter

What CAN be Patented?

GMOs (Microorganisms) Recombinant Proteins Biotech Processes Vaccines (Novel) Diagnostic Kits Cell Lines (Non-human) cDNA Gene Sequences (with utility) Modified Animals (varies) Stem Cell Lines

Microorganisms

Following Diamond v. Chakrabarty (1980) and India's 2002 amendment, genetically modified microorganisms with industrial utility are patentable. India explicitly allows this via Sec. 3(j).

Biotechnological Processes

Novel methods of producing biologics, fermentation processes, gene editing protocols, and purification techniques are broadly patentable if they satisfy the three criteria.

cDNA (Complementary DNA)

After Myriad Genetics (2013), cDNA (synthetically created from mRNA, not naturally occurring) remains patentable in the US because it does not exist in nature.

Vaccines & Biologics

Novel vaccines (e.g., mRNA vaccines), monoclonal antibodies, and therapeutic proteins developed through biotech processes are patentable subject matter in most jurisdictions.

What CANNOT be Patented?

Natural Phenomena Human Beings Laws of Nature Natural DNA Sequences Traditional Knowledge Plant Varieties (India) Animals (EU strictly) Mere Discoveries Methods of Treatment

Section 3(b) — Morality / Public Order

Inventions contrary to public order or morality, or those causing serious prejudice to human, animal, or plant life or to the environment are excluded. Many "designer baby" technologies fall here.

Section 3(c) — Mere Discoveries

The mere discovery of a scientific principle or abstract theory is not patentable. Finding that a wild plant cures fever without creating a new formulation is a discovery, not an invention.

Section 3(j) — Plants, Animals & Biological Processes

Plants and animals in whole or any part (other than microorganisms) and essentially biological processes for production of plants and animals are excluded. However, microorganisms and non-biological/microbiological processes may be patented.

Section 3(p) — Traditional Knowledge

Inventions which are traditional knowledge or aggregations of known properties of traditionally known components are excluded. India's TKDL has documented 34 million pages of traditional knowledge, successfully opposing 200+ biopiracy patent applications globally.

05 — Legal Framework

International Legal Framework

1970

Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) — Administered by WIPO, the PCT allows inventors to file a single international patent application effective in 157+ countries. Crucial for biotech companies with global portfolios.

1973

European Patent Convention (EPC) — Established the European Patent Office (EPO). Article 53(b) EPC excludes essentially biological processes; Art. 53(a) excludes inventions against public order or morality.

1977

Budapest Treaty on Microorganisms — Enables a single deposit of a microorganism with an International Depositary Authority (IDA) to satisfy deposit requirements for all contracting states. India is a signatory. Dramatically simplifies patent procedures for biotech inventions involving microorganisms.

1995

TRIPS Agreement (WTO) — Article 27(1) requires patents be available in all fields of technology. Article 27(3)(b) allows members to exclude plants and animals (except microorganisms) and essentially biological processes. India became a TRIPS signatory in 1995 upon joining the WTO.

1998

EU Biotech Directive 98/44/EC — Specifically addresses patentability of biotechnological inventions in Europe. Allows gene patents with specific utility; prohibits patents on human cloning, germline gene therapy, and commercial uses of human embryos.

2001

Doha Declaration on TRIPS and Public Health — Affirmed that TRIPS shall not prevent members from taking measures to protect public health. Critical for compulsory licensing of biotech medicines in developing nations.

WTO — World Trade Organization

Administers TRIPS. Provides dispute resolution for IP-related trade disputes and monitors compliance of 164 member states with minimum IP standards.

WIPO — World Intellectual Property Organization

UN agency administering the PCT and Budapest Treaty. Conducts capacity building for developing nations on biotech IP and manages norms on genetic resources.

India's Three Landmark Amendments

  • 1999 (First Amendment): Introduced product patent applications in mailbox provisions; complied with Art. 70(8) of TRIPS.
  • 2002 (Second Amendment): Extended patent term to 20 years; allowed patenting of biotechnological and microbiological products; India ratified the Budapest Treaty.
  • 2005 (Third Amendment): Eliminated Section 5 (process-only patents for drugs/chemicals); allowed product patents; inserted Section 3(d) to prevent evergreening.
06 — Key Case Laws

Landmark Cases in Biotech Patent Law

Landmark — USA · 1980
Diamond v. Chakrabarty
447 U.S. 303 (1980) | US Supreme Court

Ananda Chakrabarty developed a genetically engineered Pseudomonas bacterium capable of breaking down crude oil — useful for oil spill treatment. The USPTO rejected the patent, arguing living organisms are not patentable.

Held (5-4): A live, human-made organism is patentable subject matter. Famous formulation: "anything under the sun that is made by man" can be patented, provided it meets the statutory criteria.

Significance: Opened the floodgates for modern biotechnology patents. Established that the distinction between living and non-living matter is not determinative — what matters is whether the subject matter is a product of human ingenuity.

Landmark — USA · 2013
Association for Molecular Pathology v. Myriad Genetics, Inc.
569 U.S. 576 (2013) | US Supreme Court

Myriad Genetics held patents on BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes (mutations in which dramatically increase breast and ovarian cancer risk), monopolising testing at $3,000–$4,000 per test and preventing second opinions.

Held (9-0 unanimous): Naturally occurring DNA segments are products of nature — not patentable, even when isolated. However, cDNA (synthetically created, not found in nature) is patentable.

Principle: Discovery of a naturally occurring thing, however innovative the isolation technique, does not transform it into a patentable invention. The critical question is whether the claimed material is markedly different from what exists in nature.

Landmark — India · 2013
Novartis AG v. Union of India
(2013) 6 SCC 1 | Supreme Court of India

Novartis sought a patent for the beta-crystalline form of imatinib mesylate (cancer drug Gleevec). Granting the patent would have blocked cheap generic versions from Indian manufacturers supplying the developing world.

Held: Patent rejected under Section 3(d) — the new polymorph did not demonstrate significantly enhanced therapeutic efficacy compared to the known substance. Improved bioavailability alone does not equal enhanced efficacy.

Significance: Upheld India's strict anti-evergreening provision; affirmed Section 3(d) is TRIPS-compliant; protected India's role as the "pharmacy of the developing world."

India · 2002
Dimminaco AG v. Controller of Patents & Designs
AIR 2002 Cal 282 | Calcutta High Court

Dimminaco (Swiss company) sought a patent for a process for manufacturing a live vaccine against Bursitis. The Controller rejected it, contending a process ending in a living organism was not patentable.

Held: A process resulting in a living end product (a live vaccine) is patentable if it satisfies novelty, inventive step, and industrial applicability.

Significance: First Indian judicial pronouncement recognising biotech processes producing living organisms can be patented; paved the way for the 2002 amendment.

USA — Bioethics · 1990
Moore v. Regents of University of California
793 P.2d 479 (Cal. 1990)

John Moore's cancerous spleen cells were used to develop a profitable cell line without his knowledge. The University held a patent on the cell line worth millions.

Held: Moore did not have a property right in his excised cells, but the doctors had a fiduciary duty to disclose their economic interests. The University's patent on the cell line was valid.

Significance: Raises profound questions about informed consent, bodily autonomy, and commercialisation of human biological material — central to the bioethics of biotech patenting.

07 — Patent Strategies

Patent Strategies in Biotechnology

Patent Thickets

A patent thicket is a dense web of overlapping patents covering multiple aspects of a single technology, making it extremely difficult and expensive for competitors to enter the market without licensing. The CRISPR gene editing landscape — with competing patents from UC Berkeley, MIT, and the Broad Institute — is a prime contemporary example.

Problem: Patent thickets can paradoxically stifle innovation by making it too expensive or legally risky for new entrants to work in a field, even after core patents expire — the opposite of the patent system's stated purpose.

Core vs. Secondary Patents

Core (Pioneer) Patents

Cover the primary molecule, gene, or mechanism itself. These are the most valuable and typically filed first. Example: AbbVie's core patent on adalimumab (Humira) — once the world's best-selling drug.

Secondary (Satellite) Patents

Cover formulations, delivery mechanisms, dosage regimes, manufacturing processes, or specific uses. Strategically filed before core patents expire to extend effective market exclusivity — the evergreening strategy.

Case Study: Humira Patent Fortress

AbbVie filed over 100+ patents protecting Humira — covering the antibody itself, formulation, concentration, delivery device, manufacturing process, disease indications, and combination therapies. This "patent fortress" delayed biosimilar competition in the US until 2023, generating over $200 billion in cumulative sales. Critics call this the quintessential example of patent strategy being used as a barrier to competition rather than an innovation incentive.

Continuation Patents

Filing continuation applications allows companies to pursue new claims from the same original disclosure as technology evolves — keeping prosecution alive for years.

Divisional Applications

When an application contains multiple inventions, divisional filings allow each to be pursued separately — multiplying patent coverage from a single R&D breakthrough.

Patent Pooling

Companies cross-license patents, reducing litigation risk. The Medicines Patent Pool (MPP) uses this concept for HIV/HCV/TB drugs to improve access in developing nations.

Strategic Filing Timing

The US America Invents Act (2011) shifted from first-to-invent to first-to-file, intensifying the race to file before competitors publish research.

08 — Challenges & Criticisms

Challenges and Criticisms of Biotech Patents

1. Ethical Concerns — Patenting Life

Should living organisms marginally different from natural forms be treated as inventions subject to monopoly rights? Critics argue that patenting genes or modified organisms commodifies life itself, violating the ethical principle that life forms are part of the common heritage of humankind. The Myriad case raised this directly — can a corporation "own" a gene that occurs in human bodies?

2. Evergreening

Evergreening is the practice of filing patents on trivial modifications to existing drugs — new salt forms, polymorphs, dosage regimens — to extend market exclusivity without substantial therapeutic innovation. India's Section 3(d) directly targets this, as upheld in Novartis v. UOI (2013).

3. Patent Trolls (Non-Practicing Entities)

Patent trolls hold patents purely to extract licensing fees through litigation, without producing any products or conducting research. In biotech, diagnostic and research tool patents can threaten clinical laboratories and university researchers — chilling innovation rather than promoting it.

4. Access to Medicines

High-cost biologic medicines protected by layered patent strategies remain inaccessible to patients in developing countries. A course of treatment with Keytruda (pembrolizumab) can cost $150,000+ annually. Compulsory licensing (Art. 31, TRIPS; Doha Declaration 2001) provides a mechanism, but is rarely invoked due to diplomatic pressure.

5. Biopiracy

Companies filing patents on inventions based on traditional knowledge without fair benefit-sharing constitute biopiracy. The turmeric patent (US 5,401,504) and the neem patent controversy are classic examples where India successfully challenged and revoked patents via the TKDL.

09 — Alternatives to Patents

Alternatives to Patent Protection

Trade Secrets

Companies may protect biotech inventions as trade secrets — avoiding disclosure requirements and gaining potentially unlimited protection duration. In biotech, manufacturing processes for biologics (cell culture media, fermentation conditions) are commonly protected as trade secrets. Protection is lost if independently discovered or reverse-engineered.

Regulatory Exclusivity (Data Exclusivity)

Separate from patents, regulatory agencies like the US FDA grant data exclusivity preventing competitors from relying on originator clinical trial data (5 years for NCEs, 12 years for biologics under US BPCIA). India notably does not have a comprehensive data exclusivity regime — a matter of ongoing policy debate.

Plant Breeders' Rights / Sui Generis Systems

India enacted the Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights (PPV&FR) Act, 2001 — uniquely also protecting farmers' traditional rights to save, use, and exchange seeds, balancing innovation with food sovereignty.

Trademarks and Branding

Brand names (Glivec, Humira) remain protected as trademarks indefinitely. Companies leverage brand loyalty and physician familiarity to maintain market share against biosimilars even after patents expire.

Open Source Biology / Patent Pledges

The Biological Open Source (BiOS) initiative allows free use of patents for non-commercial research. During COVID-19, companies pledged not to enforce vaccine patents in low-income countries — demonstrating voluntary access mechanisms as an alternative to compulsory licensing.

10 — Future of Biotech Patents

The Future: Emerging Challenges

AI-Generated Inventions

AI systems are increasingly generating novel biotech inventions — identifying drug candidates, designing novel proteins (DeepMind's AlphaFold). The DABUS case (Thaler v. Vidal, 2022, US) raised: can an AI be named as an inventor? US, EU, India, and UK courts all answered no — the Patent Act requires human inventors. But the policy question remains: who owns AI-generated biotech innovations, and how should the law adapt?

CRISPR and Synthetic Biology

CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing — itself the subject of one of the most expensive patent disputes in history (UC Berkeley vs. Broad Institute/MIT) — enables precise genome modification. Synthetic biology goes further, designing entirely new genetic circuits and artificial life forms. Patent systems designed for mechanical inventions create immense legal uncertainty about enablement, claim scope, and non-obviousness in this context.

mRNA Technology and Pandemic Preparedness

COVID-19 vaccines from Moderna and Pfizer/BioNTech are protected by complex mRNA technology patent estates. The Moderna-NIH dispute over co-inventorship raised questions about government ownership of pandemic-critical biotech. Future pandemics will test whether patent exclusivity systems can respond quickly enough to global health emergencies.

Diagnostic Method Patents

After Mayo v. Prometheus (2012) and Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank (2014), diagnostic methods based on natural correlations face severe patentability challenges in the US — potentially leaving precision medicine innovations unprotected.

Biodiversity & Nagoya Protocol

The Nagoya Protocol (2014) requires benefit-sharing for genetic resources. Companies accessing genetic resources from biodiverse nations must demonstrate compliance — or risk invalidation of resulting patents.

Fundamental Policy Challenge: The tension in biotech patents remains unresolved — how do we maintain strong enough incentives for private innovation while ensuring that life-saving innovations remain accessible to all of humanity, especially in low-income nations?
11 — Conclusion

Conclusion: Balancing Innovation and Public Access

Biotechnology has become one of the most transformative fields in human history — offering solutions to diseases, hunger, and environmental degradation. The patent system has been its indispensable companion, providing the financial and legal infrastructure for R&D investment. Yet this partnership is increasingly strained by the tensions inherent in treating life itself as intellectual property.

"Biotechnological patenting was implemented to safeguard inventors' rights and interests. A biotechnological innovation, stemming from human creativity applied to a biological process, deserves patent protection. However, it is essential to ensure that the invention does not compromise moral values or ethics."

The core challenge for the 21st century is this: patents were designed to reward human ingenuity and promote public welfare — but in biotechnology, they are sometimes wielded to restrict access to medicines, suppress competition through thickets, and commodify living organisms of doubtful human "creation."

Policy Implications

  • Stronger Section 3(d)-type provisions should be adopted globally to prevent evergreening of biotech innovations.
  • Compulsory licensing mechanisms under TRIPS must be made more practically accessible to developing nations during health crises.
  • TKDL-type systems should be internationally recognised as prior art to combat biopiracy globally.
  • AI inventorship frameworks must be developed urgently to prevent legal uncertainty from stifling AI-driven drug discovery.
  • Patent pooling and open innovation models should be incentivised for pandemic preparedness and neglected tropical disease research.
  • Benefit-sharing under the Nagoya Protocol must be integrated into patent grant procedures worldwide.

Ultimately, as the Supreme Court of India recognised in the Novartis judgment, patent law in biotechnology must serve its fundamental utilitarian purpose: the greatest benefit to the greatest number. Biological materials created through human ingenuity deserve protection — but that protection must always be weighed against humanity's collective stake in access to the building blocks of life itself.
